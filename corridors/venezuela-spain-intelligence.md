## ⚠️ Jurisdiction Risk Flag

Venezuela is currently subject to **EU restrictive measures** under Council Regulation (EU) 2017/2063, extended through January 2026 (and likely renewed). These measures include: individual asset freezes and travel bans on designated persons, arms and related material embargo, and restrictions on surveillance technology exports.

These are **targeted individual/entity sanctions**, not a blanket trade embargo — bilateral trade with Venezuela remains legal and is active (€497M Spain–Venezuela in Jan–Oct 2025). However:

- **Any Venezuelan counterparty must undergo enhanced KYC/AML screening** before CBB engages.
- **Any deal involving Venezuelan state entities (PDVSA, government ministries, state banks) is an immediate escalation** to Armando and legal@.
- The political situation (Maduro's arrest in early 2026, contested regime) adds material legal uncertainty around counterparty standing and contract enforceability.
- US OFAC sanctions on Venezuela remain in force and may affect USD-denominated transactions or US-nexus entities — relevant if counterparties have US connections.

**Status for CBB:** This corridor is viable for research and framework development. No counterparty engagement until Armando reviews and approves the approach. All deals would require enhanced due diligence.

## Corridor Overview

### Why Venezuela→Spain is CBB's Natural First Corridor

The Venezuela–Spain corridor is not primarily a trade data play — it is a **diaspora and human capital corridor**. An estimated 300,000–500,000 Venezuelans live in Spain, concentrated in Madrid, Valencia, and Barcelona. Many are educated professionals, entrepreneurs, and business owners who retain strong commercial ties to Venezuela and family networks there.

This diaspora is the corridor. The commercial opportunity for CBB is not in routing commodities — it is in serving Venezuelan entrepreneurs and professional-class individuals who need:

- Spanish/EU business formation (SL, SLU)
- EU regulatory entry and compliance navigation
- Cross-border investment structuring
- Professional services market access

This maps directly onto CBB's LAND, OPERATE, and PEOPLE service lines — **and** onto the TRADE Principal Model as the diaspora builds commercial bridges back to Venezuela or onward into broader EU markets.

## Political and Regulatory Context

### Political Transition Risk

As of early 2026, Venezuela is in acute political transition following Maduro's arrest. Key uncertainties:

- Counterparty standing: Venezuelan company registrations and ownership may be contested or subject to restructuring.
- Contract enforceability: Any deal with a Venezuelan entity is exposed to jurisdictional risk — Venezuelan courts remain unreliable for foreign creditors.
- Sanctions trajectory: US policy under the Trump administration has signalled possible partial OFAC relaxation if Venezuela cooperates on oil sector reopening. EU sanctions review is due in January 2026 (outcome pending at time of writing).

**CBB position:** The transition is early-stage. The infrastructure for CBB to engage Venezuelan counterparties on commercial deals does not yet exist in reliable form. The **diaspora corridor** (Venezuelan entrepreneurs already in Spain) is a cleaner, lower-risk path.

### EU Sanctions (Regulation 2017/2063)

- Targeted asset freezes and travel bans: ~70 individuals designated as of January 2025 update (Implementing Regulations 2025/42 and 2025/44)
- Arms embargo: complete
- Surveillance technology export restriction: specific categories listed in Annex II
- No sector-wide trade embargo — commercial trade is legal

**Practical implication for CBB:** Screen every Venezuelan counterparty against the EU consolidated sanctions list before any engagement. State-adjacent entities (PDVSA, Banco de Venezuela, SENIAT) require escalation.

## The CBB Opportunity: Three Lanes

### Lane 1 — Venezuelan Diaspora in Spain (Immediate, Low Risk)

**Profile:** Venezuelan professionals and entrepreneurs already resident in Spain, seeking EU business formation, banking access, and professional services market entry.

- **CBB service fit:** LAND (SL formation), OPERATE (compliance, accounting, admin), PEOPLE (hiring, work permits for returning family)
- **TRADE overlay:** As diaspora businesses grow, they will need cross-border structuring for Venezuela-linked revenue or supply chains — this is where TRADE engagement begins, at Tier 1
- **Regulatory risk:** Low — these are Spain-resident individuals operating under Spanish law
- **Counterparty credibility:** Evaluable — track records are documentable

**Recommended first action:** Marco's sales pipeline should include Venezuelan diaspora entrepreneurs as a named target segment. I will flag this to him in the next daily peer note.

### Lane 2 — Venezuelan Food & Beverage Exports (Medium Term, Conditional)

**Profile:** Venezuelan private-sector F&B producers (cacao, coffee, rum, tropical fish) seeking EU market entry as sanctions environment clarifies.

- Venezuela has world-class cacao genetics (Criollo variety), premium rum heritage, and artisanal food producers who have survived by exporting through informal or third-country channels
- Post-transition, a credible private-sector F&B export corridor is plausible — but requires political stabilisation and sanctions trajectory clarity
- **CBB service fit:** TRADE Tier 1 (market access intelligence, distributor identification, regulatory pathway) → Tier 2 (deal structuring for first-mover EU distribution agreements)
- **Regulatory risk:** Medium — TARIC 03 (fish) and TARIC 22 (beverages/spirits) trade is active and legal. SPS/food safety compliance at EU border is the primary regulatory friction point.
- **Counterparty risk:** Medium-high — Venezuelan private sector counterparties need careful KYC

**Recommended first action:** Research which Venezuelan cacao and rum producers are currently exporting to EU markets through third countries (Colombia, Panama, Trinidad). These are the credible counterparties. Not ready for active engagement — this is 6–12 month positioning.

### Lane 3 — Spanish Capital into Venezuela (Long Term, High Caution)

**Profile:** Spanish corporates or family offices with legacy Venezuela ties seeking to reposition for post-transition investment.

- Several major Spanish companies (Telefónica, BBVA, Repsol) had historical Venezuela exposure and exited or were expropriated. Some may revisit.
- CBB's role would be intelligence and deal architecture — not principal capital deployment
- **Risk:** This is a speculative, long-horizon lane contingent on political stabilisation that has not yet occurred
- **Position:** Monitor only. No client conversations or deal activity until Armando reviews.
